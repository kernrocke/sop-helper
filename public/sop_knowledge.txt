1.  Introduction

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized
processes for the detection and notification of Events Supposedly
Attributable to Vaccination or Immunization (ESAVI) in Trinidad and
Tobago. Ensuring the safety of vaccines is paramount to maintaining
public trust in immunization programs and safeguarding the health of the
population.

This document aims to provide clear and concise guidelines for
healthcare workers, surveillance officers, and other relevant personnel
involved in the national ESAVI surveillance system. By adhering to these
procedures, we can ensure timely and accurate detection and notification
of ESAVI, facilitate prompt investigation and appropriate management of
cases, and contribute to the ongoing monitoring of vaccine safety in
Trinidad and Tobago.

This SOP is aligned with the international guidelines set out by the
World Health Organization (WHO) and the Pan American Health Organization
(PAHO). It has been adapted to the specific context of the national
immunization program, promoting a robust and effective ESAVI
surveillance system in Trinidad and Tobago.

2.  Purpose

The purpose of this SOP is to describe the process and workflow for
detecting and reporting of events supposedly attributable to vaccination
or immunization in Trinidad and Tobago.

3.  Scope

This procedure applies for detecting ESAVI by healthcare workers and,
notifying and reporting to the sub-national and national level.

4.  Pre-requisites or Assumptions

    i.  National reporting form meeting the international and regional
        standards.

    ii. All healthcare workers have received training on the ESAVI case
        definition.

5.  Reference Documentation

    i.  National ESAVI surveillance manual

    ii. National EPI manual

    iii. Regional ESAVI surveillance manual

    iv. National Public Health and Medical Act Chapters 28-31

6.  Roles & Responsibility

    i.  Healthcare workers – Any health professional who works within a
        healthcare setting and contributes to the delivery of healthcare
        services.

    ii. ESAVI focal point – A designated individual responsible for
        coordinating and managing activities related to ESAVI within a
        specific area (healthcare facility, county or national level)

    iii. Surveillance Nurse – A healthcare worker (nurse) who plays a
         key role in the systematic collection, analysis and
         interpretation of health-related data

7.  Abbreviations

    i.  ESAVI – Events Supposedly Attributable to Vaccination or
        Immunization

    ii. CMOH – County Medical Officer of Health

    iii. CMO – Chief Medical Officer

    iv. EPI – Expanded Programme in Immunization

    v.  WHO – World Health Organization

    vi. PAHO – Pan American Health Organization

    vii. MoH – Ministry of Health

    viii. HcW – Healthcare worker

8.  Definitions

    i.  ESAVI – Any untoward medical occurrence which follows
        immunization, and which does not necessarily have a causal
        relationship with the usage of the vaccine. The adverse event
        may be any unfavorable or unintended sign, abnormal laboratory
        finding, symptom, or disease.

    ii. Detection – Process of identifying health events of interest
        based on a pre-specified criteria or definition

    iii. Notification – This refers to the act of communicating
         information of any adverse health events to health authorities
         or interested stakeholders.

    iv. Reporting – This refers to the acting of documenting and sharing
        information of an adverse event post vaccination to health
        authorities or interested stakeholders.

9.  Categorization of ESAVI

ESAVIs are categorized based on the level of seriousness. For an ESAVI
case to be considered serious, the any following conditions must be met:

1.  Causes the death of the vaccinated individual.

2.  Poses an imminent danger to the life of the vaccinated individual.

3.  Requires hospitalization or prolongs a hospital stay.

4.  Causes of persistent or significant disability or incapacity.

5.  Is suspected of having caused a congenital abnormality or
    stillbirth.

6.  Is suspected of having caused an abortion.

 

For an ESAVI case to be considered non-serious, the following conditions
must be met:

1.  Does NOT cause death.

2.  Does NOT require hospitalization.

3.  Does NOT cause disability or long-term disorder(s).

4.  Does NOT endanger vaccinated person, embryo, fetus, or newborn.

5.  Is resolved without medical intervention or with symptomatic
    treatment.

10. Detection of ESAVI

In the passive surveillance system ESAVI are identified by either the
vaccinated person, by their family or by a healthcare professional.
ESAVI are detected using the standardized case definition (see 6.i) and
are categorized based on its seriousness. Any change in the health
status of the vaccinated person within the first 30 days post
vaccination may be suspected as a result of the vaccine however the
occurrence of an event after the first 30 days does not exclude the
possibility of it being associated with the vaccine.

11. Notification of ESAVI

All healthcare providers must notify any ESAVI to the designated local
ESAVI focal point at the health facility and should be reported
utilizing the standardized ESAVI reporting form. This notification
facilitates timely investigation and data analysis, contributing to the
ongoing monitoring of vaccine safety and ensuring appropriate public
health responses.

12. Reporting of ESAVI

All adverse events post vaccination/immunization identified or observed
whether expected or unexpected should be notified to the ESAVI focal
point (supervisory surveillance nurse) at the healthcare institution.
Identified cases should be reported in the national reporting form for
serious and non-serious ESAVI and should be submitted to the supervisory
surveillance nurse according to the criteria for reporting serious and
non-serious ESAVI cases.

13. Reporting Channels

a)  Paper-based reporting – All ESAVI reporting forms should be located
    among the MoH surveillance forms at the healthcare facility.
    Following of the completion of the paper-based ESAVI reporting form,
    the healthcare worker will submit the form to the ESAVI focal point
    at the healthcare institution which will be reviewed for
    completeness and accuracy. Once approved a copy of the form will be
    sent the CMOH office to be reviewed by the surveillance team before
    another copy of the form is sent to EPI manager office at the
    Ministry of Health. Scanned copies of the completed reporting form
    can be sent via email or via approved mobile messaging applications.

b)  Electronic reporting - Healthcare workers should be able to
    electronically report ESAVI through a secure, web-based portal
    accessible via their healthcare institution's intranet or a
    designated government health website. This platform allows for
    direct data entry of patient information, vaccine details, and
    observed adverse events, ensuring timely submission to the national
    ESAVI surveillance system. The system often includes data validation
    checks and automated notifications to relevant authorities,
    streamlining the reporting and follow-up process.

1.  Introduction

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized
processes for the detection and notification of Events Supposedly
Attributable to Vaccination or Immunization (ESAVI) in Trinidad and
Tobago. Ensuring the safety of vaccines is paramount to maintaining
public trust in immunization programs and safeguarding the health of the
population.

This document aims to provide clear and concise guidelines for the
national investigation team and other relevant personnel involved in the
investigation of ESAVI. By adhering to these procedures, we can ensure
timely and accurate conduct of ESAVI investigation, facilitate prompt
feedback, appropriate management of cases, and contribute to the ongoing
monitoring of vaccine safety in Trinidad and Tobago.

This SOP is aligned with the international guidelines set out by the
World Health Organization (WHO) and the Pan American Health Organization
(PAHO). It has been adapted to the specific context of the national
immunization program, promoting a robust and effective ESAVI
surveillance system in Trinidad and Tobago.

2.  Purpose

The purpose of this SOP is to describe the process and workflow for
investigating of events supposedly attributable to vaccination or
immunization in Trinidad and Tobago.

3.  Scope

This procedure applies for the conduct of an ESAVI investigation by a
specialized team of health professionals.

4.  Pre-requisites or Assumptions

    i.  A clear and detailed ESAVI report is available

    ii. An ESAVI investigation team of health professionals is defined
        with a team lead established.

    iii. All members of the investigation team have received training on
         ESAVI investigation

    iv. All members of the ESAVI Investigation Team must have no
        affiliation to the vaccine manufacturer or distributor.

    v.  All members of the ESAVI Investigation Team must adhere to
        ethical principles in all aspects of the ESAVI investigation.

    vi. The physicians, nurses, and/or any healthcare worker involved in
        the investigation or on the investigation team should not be
        from the facility where the case was reported or involved in the
        administration of care.

    vii. Risk communication plan for vaccine safety events

    viii. Established secure communication channels that will be used to
          communicate between internal and external stakeholders.

    ix. Standardized tool for capture information for the investigation
        – National ESAVI investigation form

5.  Reference Documentation

    i.  National ESAVI surveillance manual

    ii. National EPI manual

    iii. Regional ESAVI surveillance manual

    iv. National Public Health and Medical Act Chapters 28-31

6.  Roles & Responsibility

The conduct an ESAVI investigation, the ESAVI investigation Team should
be composed of a group of multidisciplinary experts, including but not
limited to the following:

i.  Epidemiologist/Surveillance Officer

ii. Clinical Investigator (County Medical Office of Health)

iii. Pharmacovigilance Officer

iv. Public Health Specialist

v.  Immunization Specialist or healthcare professional with expertise in
    EPI, vaccine safety, or cold chain management

The composition of the Investigation Team may vary depending on the
complexity of the case, available experts, and resources, however, if
the role of the suggested healthcare professionals is not available,
designated representatives from the following departments of the MoH are
recommended:

- The Epidemiology Division

- The Pharmacovigilance Unit

- The Expanded Immunization Programme (EPI)

8.  Definitions

    i.  ESAVI – Any untoward medical occurrence which follows
        immunization, and which does not necessarily have a causal
        relationship with the usage of the vaccine. The adverse event
        may be any unfavorable or unintended sign, abnormal laboratory
        finding, symptom, or disease.

    ii. Detection – Process of identifying health events of interest
        based on a pre-specified criteria or definition

    iii. Notification – This refers to the act of communicating
         information of any adverse health events to health authorities
         or interested stakeholders.

    iv. Reporting – This refers to the acting of documenting and sharing
        information of an adverse event post vaccination to health
        authorities or interested stakeholders.

    v.  Investigation – Gathering accurate and comprehensive information
        about serious adverse events and events of interest, including
        the surrounding circumstances, in order to identify the causes
        and take appropriate action.

9.  ESAVI Investigation Criteria

The following conditions must be met to initiate an ESAVI investigation:

- The onset of clinical illness began after the vaccine was
  administered.

- There is no ambiguity in the clinical diagnosis of the case.

- Alternative explanations for the case have not been ruled out yet.

ESAVI investigations should be begin within the first 48 hours of case
notification.

To fully understand the event(s) that occurred and to verify the
clinical diagnosis, a complete and thorough investigation must be
conducted for the following:

i)  all serious ESAVI cases; however,

ii) non-serious cases should only be investigated in the following
    situations:

a.  Events that occur in clusters.

b.  Events that occur during a vaccine campaign.

c.  Events that have media attention surrounding the case.

d.  Events associated with vaccines from a new manufacturer.

e.  Events related to a suspected or confirmed programmatic error.

f.  When the frequency of the event is higher than expected.

g.  Unusual clinical presentation after a vaccine was administered.

h.  The occurrence of unexpected events in which the relation to the
    vaccination is unclear.

i.  Events related to a newly introduced vaccine

j.  Events related to a known vaccine with new indicators (i.e. new
    population, new schedule, prevention for a new disease, etc.)

k.  Signals: new undescribed events that were not identified in clinical
    trials.

1.  ESAVI Clusters

A cluster can be defined as two or more cases of the same or similar
events related in time, geographical location, or area, and/or vaccine
administered.

Clusters may be identified in the following ways:

- During Data Analysis: by an ongoing surveillance system that includes
  epidemiologic or statistical exercises and cluster detection
  algorithms.

- Notification: when multiple ESAVI cases with similar characteristics
  are seen at a healthcare facility.

ESAVI Cluster Classification:

- Figure 1. - “Analysis of Clusters” can be used to accurately classify
  a group of cases as a cluster.

A decision can be made to conduct only an abbreviated investigation into
the occurrence of a cluster of non-serious ESAVI under the following
circumstances:

- When there is no ambiguity as to the clinical diagnosis of the case.

- When alternative explanations for the case have been ruled out.

- When the cases do not exhibit any unusual clinical or epidemiological
  pattern.

- When the cases are not connected with the same lot of vaccine.

- When there is no evidence to suspect programmatic errors or deviations
  in vaccine quality.

2.  ESAVI Investigation Steps

This is the standard structure and flow of an ESAVI investigation:

- Define the problem

- Plan and Prepare for the Investigation

- Establish a Timeline for the sequence of events

- Investigate & Collect Data on the following:

  - The patient

  - The event

  - The suspected vaccine(s)

  - Other potentially related cases

  - Site/route of immunization, etc.

- Assess the vaccination process

- Review operational aspects of the immunization programme

- Collect Samples on the following:

  - Patient samples, guided by clinical presentation

  - Vaccine samples

- Conclusion of the Investigation

The necessary steps to follow when conducting an ESAVI Investigation are
as follows:

The ESAVI Focal Point at MoH EPI department will receive notification
from the focal point at the county medical office of health. They will
review the ESAVI reporting form and case report file and then determine
if the case meets investigation criteria.

If an investigation is needed, the ESAVI Focal Point will initiate the
investigation process by notifying the EPI Manager, ESAVI focal point at
the Ministry of Health Epidemiology Division, and any other designated
personnel.

11.1 Defining the Problem

The case investigation team lead will review the following and define
the problem:

- Who:

  - The number of people affected

  - Sex, Age, Ethnicity, etc.

  - Did they have any pre-existing health conditions?

  - Is the case isolated or are there any related or similar cases?

  - Verify if there are similar cases in non-vaccinated people

  - Were there detectable differences between those immunized and those
    who were not immunized?

- Where:

- Did the event occur at home, healthcare facility during a regular
  checkup, or in the hospital?

- How:

- Was it spontaneous, acute, chronic, or incidental?

- When:

  - Did the event occur immediately, several days, weeks, or months
    after vaccination?

  - Did the event occur after a new medication was introduced?

11.2 Case Investigation Planning & Preparation

- Identify if any additional experts are needed, as participants in the
  ESAVI Investigation or team members will vary on a case-by-case basis,
  depending on the needs of each case. The core team will consist of the
  members listed in section 6.

- Clearly outline roles and responsibilities for each investigation.

- Establish case definitions, criteria and guidelines

- Identify a spokesperson for public communication/media point of
  contact.

Tasks to complete prior to initiating an ESAVI Investigation:

- Review and verify information on and completeness of the National
  ESAVI Reporting Form and case file prior to starting the
  investigation.

  - Request the patient’s comprehensive medical history and clinical
    records.

  - Obtain any information that is missing from the ESAVI notification
    and/or investigation forms.

  - Identify all cases that need to be included into the investigation
    and obtain the relevant information.

- An effective communication plan should be developed.

  - Communication channels between internal and external stakeholders
    must be established.

- Ensure that all team members are capturing data through a standardized
  form-based tool.

- Verify the diagnosis or establish a corresponding diagnosis(es).

- In the event of death, an autopsy should be conducted and documented.

  - If an autopsy is not available, a verbal autopsy by a trained
    professional should be performed.

11.2 Conducting the Case Investigation

11.2.1 Investigation & Data Collection: The event

- Establish a timeline (clearly define the start and end of the event).

- Document the history of events in chronological order.

- Obtain a detailed description of the sequence of events/ clinical
  manifestations, response to treatment.

11.2.2 Investigation & Data Collection: The Vaccinated Individual

When interviewing the affected or vaccinated person, be sure to obtain
information pertaining to the following:

a.  Demographic data

b.  Recent clinical history

c.  Pathological history

d.  Medical-surgical history

e.  Family history

f.  Vaccine history

g.  Gynecology and Obstetrics history

h.  Pharmacological and Toxicological history

i.  Socioeconomic, environmental conditions

11.2.3 Investigation & Data Collection: The Vaccine

The ESAVI Investigation Team is expected to observe and document the
following activities at vaccination sites:

- How vaccines are handled

- The condition of vaccine vials

- Vaccine distribution and disposal

- Batch numbers and expiration dates

- If vials are labelled

- If other drugs stored with the vaccines and diluents

- How vaccines are placed in cold chain

- Vaccination procedures:

  - Vaccine administration techniques

  - Reconstitution procedure

  - Time between reconstitution and administration

  - The number of vaccines administered within a specific timeframe

- Verify the storage conditions of the vaccine, diluent, and the device
  for administering the vaccine.

- Verify the shipping conditions and protocol from manufacturer into
  country

The ESAVI Investigation Team is expected to complete the following
activities:

Interviews to conduct:

- Patient

- Parent(s)/guardian(s)

- Caregiver(s)

- Healthcare worker(s) who administered vaccine

- Healthcare worker(s) who provided care/treatment

- Healthcare worker(s) who completed reporting form

Visits to conduct:

- Vaccination site

- Vaccine storage

- Residence and/or Family Home

- When conducting a visit to a patient's home, complete the following
  activities:

- Confirm the chronological order and history of events reported by
  interviewing family.

- Observe the environmental and living conditions of the home.

- Inquire about the use of alternative medicines (i.e. bush medicine).

*It’s imperative to be sensitive to the family and speak in layman
terms.

- Healthcare Facility that patient received care

- Hospital (if patient was examined at hospital, obtained treatment at
  hospital, or was hospitalized.)

Community Involvement:

There is a need to expand the investigation to the community to:

- Determine if other individuals in the community have experienced
  similar illnesses, ascertain the vaccination status of the affected
  individuals.

- Determine if affected individuals received the vaccine from the same
  vaccination site/ healthcare facility.

- Observe environmental risks and potential toxic exposures.

11.3 ESAVI Investigation Conclusion

The ESAVI Investigation Team will meet to complete the following
activities:

- Establish the timeline of the case

- Review and discuss all epidemiological, clinical, and laboratory
  findings

- Formulate Hypothesis on the likely/possible cause(s) of the event

- Test Hypothesis

- Reach a provisional conclusion on the cause of the case

- Generate and submit the one final and official ESAVI Investigation
  Form to the EPI Division

- All ESAVI case information can be recorded on a paper-based
  investigation form, however it must be transcribed and stored into a
  ESAVI case investigation database.

- The EPI Manager or designated personnel will review the case
  investigation form and final report and submit the same to the Chief
  Medical Officer (CMO).

- The EPI Manager will notify the sub-committee of the National
  Immunization Technical Advisory Group (NITAG) which focuses on vaccine
  safety of any ESAVI investigation cases that requires causality
  assessment and final classification.

- Upon completion of the ESAVI investigation, The EPI Division will
  create a digital folder with the following information necessary for
  the NITAG to conduct causality assessment and final classification:

  - ESAVI reporting form

  - ESAVI investigation form

  - ESAVI case investigation report

  - Case’s clinical case records and information, laboratory findings,
    diagnostic reports, radiology imaging

  - Valid case diagnosis

  - autopsy report (where applicable),

  - Photographs of the case’s community and household

  - and any relevant or requested information that should be submitted
    to the sub-committee of the NITAG for causality assessment and final
    classification.

1.  Introduction

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized
processes for the causality assessment and final classification of
Events Supposedly Attributable to Vaccination or Immunization (ESAVI)
cases in Trinidad and Tobago. Ensuring the safety of vaccines is
paramount to maintaining public trust in immunization programs and
safeguarding the health of the population.

This document aims to provide clear and concise guidelines for the
sub-committee of the National Immunization Technical Advisory Group
(NITAG) focusing on vaccine safety. By adhering to these procedures, we
can ensure timely and accurate conduct of ESAVI causality assessment and
final classification, facilitate prompt feedback, appropriate management
of cases, and contribute to the ongoing monitoring of vaccine safety in
Trinidad and Tobago.

This SOP is aligned with the international guidelines set out by the
World Health Organization (WHO) and the Pan American Health Organization
(PAHO). It has been adapted to the specific context of the national
immunization program, promoting a robust and effective ESAVI
surveillance system in Trinidad and Tobago (See figure 1)

2.  Purpose

The purpose of this SOP is to describe the process and workflow for the
conduct of causality assessment and final classification of events
supposedly attributable to vaccination or immunization cases in Trinidad
and Tobago.

3.  Scope

This procedure applies for the conduct of an ESAVI causality assessment
and final classification by a specialized committee of health
professionals.

4.  Pre-requisites or Assumptions

    i.  A clear and detailed ESAVI report is available

    ii. A clear and detailed ESAVI investigation has been completed.
        Note: Premature assessment with inadequate information may
        mislead the classification of the event.

    iii. A sub-committee of the NITAG of health professionals is defined
         focusing on vaccine safety

    iv. All members of the vaccine safety committee have received
        training on ESAVI detection, investigation, causality assessment
        and final classification

    v.  All members of the vaccine safety committee must have no
        affiliation to the vaccine manufacturer or distributor or
        national governmental agencies.

    vi. All members of the vaccine safety committee must adhere to
        ethical principles in all aspects of the ESAVI causality
        assessment and final classification.

    vii. No member of the vaccine safety committee should have
         previously involved with the ESAVI investigation nor on the
         national investigation team nor involved in the administration
         of care of the patient.

    viii. Standardized tool for capture information for the causality
          assessment & final classification

    ix. Established a conflict of interest and confidentiality agreement
        with all members of the vaccine safety committee.

    x.  All details of the case should be available at the time of the
        causality assessment. Supporting documents related to the
        investigation should be included in the case dossier file.

    xi. There must be a valid diagnosis for the unfavorable or
        unintended sign, symptom, abnormal laboratory finding or disease
        in question.

    xii. All vaccines and medicines that were administered before the
         event should be identified and documented.

8.  Definitions

    i.  ESAVI – Any untoward medical occurrence which follows
        immunization, and which does not necessarily have a causal
        relationship with the usage of the vaccine. The adverse event
        may be any unfavorable or unintended sign, abnormal laboratory
        finding, symptom, or disease.

    ii. Detection – Process of identifying health events of interest
        based on a pre-specified criteria or definition

    iii. Notification – This refers to the act of communicating
         information of any adverse health events to health authorities
         or interested stakeholders.

    iv. Reporting – This refers to the acting of documenting and sharing
        information of an adverse event post vaccination to health
        authorities or interested stakeholders.

    v.  Investigation – Gathering accurate and comprehensive information
        about serious adverse events and events of interest, including
        the surrounding circumstances, in order to identify the causes
        and take appropriate action.

    vi. Causal association – A cause and effect relationship between a
        causative factor and a disease with no other factors intervening
        in the process.

    vii. Causality Assessment – The systematic review of data about an
         ESAVI which aims to determine the likelihood of a causal
         association between the event and the vaccine(s) received.

    viii. Vaccine product-related event: An ESAVI that is caused or
          precipitated by a vaccine due to one or more of the inherent
          properties of the vaccine product, whether the active
          ingredient or any or the vaccine component

    ix. Vaccine quality deviation related event : A ESAVI caused by
        deviations from vaccine quality specification including in
        devices used for vaccine administration, manufacturing
        processes, storage or the distribution chain

    x.  Event related to a programmatic error: A ESAVI caused by
        deviations from vaccine quality specification including in
        devices used for vaccine administration, manufacturing
        processes, storage or the distribution chain

    xi. Stress-related event occurring immediately before, during or
        immediately after vaccination: An ESAVI caused by stress about
        the vaccination process and related sociocultural factors.

    xii. Coincidental event: An ESAVI that is not caused by the vaccine,
         a programmatic error or immunization stress, but which has a
         temporal relationship with the vaccine

    xiii. Non-classifiable event : Such events are defined operationally
          when, given a lack of information the event cannot be
          classified in any other category.

9.  Case selection for Causality Assessment & Final Classification

The following conditions must be meet to initiate an ESAVI causality
assessment:

a)  Serious ESAVI (See definition in SOP – detection & reporting)

b)  ESAVI cases related to new vaccines or that have occurred in certain
    populations such as children, adolescents and pregnant women.

c)  ESAVI case clusters, regardless of their severity

d)  The occurrence of events are above the expected rate or of unusual
    severity

e)  Vaccine safety signals generated as a result of individual or
    clustered cases at any geographic level

f)  ESAVI cases that have the potential to cause a media crisis or that
    have already triggered one.

g)  ESAVI cases in which there is doubt about the need to recall a batch
    of vaccine a vaccine.

h)  Special cases

    i.  ESAVI that may have been caused by programmatic error

    ii. Events causing significant parental or community concern

    iii. Significant events of unexplained cause occurring up after
         vaccination (and not listed in product fact sheets or labels)

10. Causality Assessment Steps

This is the standard structure and flow of the causality assessment
process

- Evaluation of eligibility

- Verification checklist

- Causality assessment algorithm

- Classification of the event

The necessary steps to follow when conducting an ESAVI causality
assessment are as follows:

The ESAVI Focal Point at MoH EPI department will collate all of the
information obtained from the ESAVI case investigation into a case
dossier. All documents related to the case will then be sent to the
secretariat of the national vaccine safety committee for review. Once
approved all documents will be shared with the vaccine safety committee
for conducting the causality assessment procedure. Note: Causality
assessment should be performed for each vaccine administered and for
each sign, symptom, abnormal laboratory finding or disease.

10.1 Evaluation of eligibility

- Valid case diagnosis confirmed using standardize case definition (Use
  Brighton Collaboration Criteria)

- Confirm sequence of events in relation to vaccine administration
  (ensure vaccine was administered before the onset of symptoms or signs
  of the event)

- Case investigation must be completed and all details of the case is
  made available.

- Create causality question

10.2 Verification checklist

- Utilization of causality assessment checklist

- Answers should include a singular response of yes, no, not known. If
  yes is selected for any question: comments and supporting evidence
  should be included.

10.3 Causality assessment algorithm

- Completed following the conclusion of the verification checklist

- All evidence is reviewed, if there is missing information additional
  measure are taken to collect the missing information

- Algorithm applied based on the responses from the checklist (See
  manual for algorithm)

10.4 Classification of the event

- Based on the findings from the causality assessment algorithm, each
  case is classified into one of the following categories (further
  details can be found in the national ESAVI surveillance manual):

  - Events that have a causal association with the vaccine or any of it
    components

    - Vaccine product-related event

    - Event related to a deviation in vaccine quality

  - Event that has a causal association with the vaccination process

    - Event related to a programmatic error

    - Event caused by stress that occurred immediately before, during or
      immediately after vaccination

  - Undetermined

    - The temporal relationship is consistent but there is not enough
      evidence to determine the vaccine is the cause

    - Qualifying factors result in conflicting trends or consistency and
      inconsistency with causal association to immunization

  - Inconsistent with causal association to vaccination (coincidental
    event)

  - Non-classifiable

11. Final report structure

    i.  Case identification: Includes the identification of the person
        affected by the event and the file number when available. Basic
        sociodemographic variables may be included.

    ii. Results of the evaluation of the information available at the
        time of the evaluation: A mention of the quantity and quality of
        the information available at the time of the causality
        evaluation will be included, with an overall opinion of the
        same.

    iii. Final classification and arguments on the classification: The
         main classification and the arguments that justify it are
         presented. In case of having arguments for more than one
         classification, it should be specified in the report and also
         include the arguments.

    iv. Recommendations on case follow-up should be included and if
        there are any on diagnosis or medical care could also be
        included.

1.  Introduction

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized
processes for the management of Events Supposedly Attributable to
Vaccination or Immunization (ESAVI) cases at the sub-national and
national levels in Trinidad and Tobago.

This document aims to provide clear and concise guide for personnel in
charge of surveillance or pharmacovigilance of ESAVI at the sub-national
and national level. By adhering to these guidelines and procedures, we
can ensure accurate and effective management of ESAVI cases to
facilitate prompt feedback and contribution to the ongoing monitoring of
vaccine safety in Trinidad and Tobago.

This SOP is aligned with the international guidelines set out by the
World Health Organization (WHO) and the Pan American Health Organization
(PAHO). It has been adapted to the specific context of the national
immunization program, promoting a robust and effective ESAVI
surveillance system in Trinidad and Tobago. The findings and
considerations identified in this individual case review should be
discussed in the inter-programmatic team and at national level
discussions.

[]

Figure 1: Trinidad and Tobago ESAVI Surveillance System Flow Diagram.

2.  Purpose

The purpose of this SOP is to describe the process and workflow for the
case management of events supposedly attributable to vaccination or
immunization cases in Trinidad and Tobago.

3.  Scope

This procedure applies for the conduct of an ESAVI causality assessment
and final classification by a specialized committee of health
professionals.

4.  Pre-requisites or Assumptions

    i.  A clear and detailed ESAVI report is available

    ii. Standardized tool for capture information for the causality
        assessment & final classification

    iii. All vaccines and medicines that were administered before the
         event should be identified and documented where possible.

5.  Reference Documentation

    i.  National ESAVI surveillance manual

    ii. National EPI manual

    iii. Regional ESAVI surveillance manual

    iv. National Public Health and Medical Act Chapters 28-31

6.  Roles & Responsibility

    i.  Healthcare workers – Any health professional who works within a
        healthcare setting and contributes to the delivery of healthcare
        services.

    ii. ESAVI focal point – A designated individual responsible for
        coordinating and managing activities related to ESAVI within a
        specific area (healthcare facility, county or national level)

    iii. Surveillance Nurse – A healthcare worker (nurse) who plays a
         key role in the systematic collection, analysis and
         interpretation of health-related data

    iv. Pharmacovigilance officer

    v.  Surveillance officer

7.  Abbreviations

    i.  ESAVI – Events Supposedly Attributable to Vaccination or
        Immunization

    ii. CMOH – County Medical Officer of Health

    iii. CMO – Chief Medical Officer

    iv. EPI – Expanded Programme in Immunization

    v.  WHO – World Health Organization

    vi. PAHO – Pan American Health Organization

    vii. MoH – Ministry of Health

    viii. HcW – Healthcare worker

    ix. UUID – Universal Unique Identification Number

8.  Definitions

    i.  ESAVI – Any untoward medical occurrence which follows
        immunization, and which does not necessarily have a causal
        relationship with the usage of the vaccine. The adverse event
        may be any unfavorable or unintended sign, abnormal laboratory
        finding, symptom, or disease.

    ii. Detection – Process of identifying health events of interest
        based on a pre-specified criteria or definition

    iii. Notification – This refers to the act of communicating
         information of any adverse health events to health authorities
         or interested stakeholders.

    iv. Reporting – This refers to the acting of documenting and sharing
        information of an adverse event post vaccination to health
        authorities or interested stakeholders via filling out a
        national reporting form for that purpose.

9.  Procedure

This procedure is defined in three phase:

1)  Receipt and initial assessment of the ESAVI report

2)  Data entry and verification

3)  Case follow-up and information gathering

1.  Introduction

This Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) outlines the standardized
processes for the management and analysis of Event Supposedly
Attributable to Vaccination or Immunization (ESAVI) data in Trinidad and
Tobago. Ensuring the safety of vaccines is paramount to maintaining
public trust in immunization programs and safeguarding the health of the
population. This document aims to provide clear and concise guidelines
for data analysts; biostatisticians; epidemiologists, and other relevant
personnel involved in the national ESAVI surveillance system. By
adhering to these procedures, we can ensure the timely and accurate
management, and effective analysis of ESAVI data, facilitating prompt
investigation and appropriate management of cases, and contributing to
the ongoing monitoring of vaccine safety in Trinidad and Tobago.

This SOP addresses the first contact with the country's data and seeks
to carry out the first quality assessment. For this process, elements of
the data validation methodology published by the statistical office of
the European Union were taken. In this methodology, data editing is
defined as "activity aimed at detecting and correcting errors in the
data". This process includes three phases:

1.  Definition of a consistent system of requirements (validation
    rules).

2.  Verification of the rules in a data set (data validation).

3.  Investigation and correction of errors (correction or imputation of
    data).

2.  Purpose

The purpose of this SOP is to describe the process and workflow for data
management and analysis of events supposedly attributable to vaccination
or immunization in Trinidad and Tobago.

3.  Scope

This procedure applies for data management and analysis of ESAVI data by
analysts; biostatisticians; epidemiologists at the national level.

4.  Pre-requisites or Assumptions

    i.  National ESAVI database

    ii. All analysts have received training on the ESAVI data management
        and analysis.

5.  Reference Documentation

    i.  National ESAVI surveillance manual

    ii. Regional ESAVI surveillance manual

6.  Roles & Responsibility

    i.  Data entry clerk- An individual with the task of transcribing
        analogue paper based records into a digital database.

    ii. Data analyst – Any professional responsible for the management
        and analysis of public health data

    iii. Data Manager – A professional with the authority to manage all
         data related to public health events. This professionals
         approves all data analysis steps, checks and balances within
         the data management process.

7.  Abbreviations

    i.  ESAVI – Events Supposedly Attributable to Vaccination or
        Immunization

    ii. CMO – Chief Medical Officer

    iii. EPI – Expanded Programme in Immunization

    iv. WHO – World Health Organization

    v.  PAHO – Pan American Health Organization

    vi. MoH – Ministry of Health

8.  Definitions

    i.  ESAVI – Any untoward medical occurrence which follows
        immunization, and which does not necessarily have a causal
        relationship with the usage of the vaccine. The adverse event
        may be any unfavorable or unintended sign, abnormal laboratory
        finding, symptom, or disease.

    ii. Reporting – This refers to the acting of documenting and sharing
        information of an adverse event post vaccination to health
        authorities or interested stakeholders.

    iii. Database – A collection of data record stored in a standard
         format

    iv. Data Discrepancy – Any data field or record that does not comply
        with formatting guidelines and rules such as data type and
        standardization codes.

    v.  Completeness - Refers to the presence of mandatory information
        within a form/variable. Also, refers to the absence of null
        values or blank space(s) within a variable when mandatory.

    vi. Standardization - Compliance with established formatting
        guidelines

    vii. Duplicate- Data element that pertains to the same fact whose
         duplicity in a record has no additional value.

    viii. Incomplete/Erroneous- Data that has missing components, has
          false data, or not following the recommended international
          coding criterion for adverse events. Additionally, a variable
          can be said to be incomplete when it has null values where
          mandatory to be non-null.

GLOSSARY OF TERMS

- Automated data adjustments: Occur because of computer actions. A
  desirable option in any system that allows compute actions is to allow
  the override of those actions at some level. Automated data adjustment
  occurs because of the systematic implementation of a data review
  method

- Data Quality Audit: A method of tracking changes in the values of a
  field and the reason and source of each change. Audit trails usually
  begin after the initial interview is complete.

- Self-correction: procedure by which use is made of some automatic
  correction rule using a computer without the need for human
  supervision. This procedure must be executed according to the needs of
  the country to guarantee that the corrected record will avoid
  interpretation errors or discrepancies with new versions of the files
  delivered to avoid introducing or letting data quality errors pass.
  The removal or substitution of incorrect records can make use of
  matching tables as required by the country or regional database.

- Correction Cycle: The time cycle in which changes (data editing) are
  made to a record according to an existing editing strategy.

- Reject Rule Set: set of all explicit and implicit reject rules
  (quality problems).

- Set of edits: set of all explicit and implicit edits that are used to
  generate a clean database before the country reviews the records.

- Error detection: activity aimed at detecting erroneous data using the
  predefined correction criteria. The correction criteria can be defined
  through various control rules (acceptance rules, check/check rules and
  correction rules).

- Analytical editing: procedure in which editing rule(s) are established
  from logical/mathematical reasoning.

- Conditional editing: editing process where the value of a field
  determines the relationship with other variables and possibly with
  itself. For example, editing the Age, Sex, and Pregnancy fields at the
  time of the ESAVI is conditional.

- Consistency edition: edition of the records with a consistency error,
  implementing the correction rule that validates the relationship
  between the variables under analysis.

- Data editing: global procedure for detecting and correcting errors
  (logical inconsistencies) in the data.

- Edition from the point: Edition that is carried out from the focal
  point of each country after the process of localization of errors.
  Editing can be part of a data entry system.

- Consistency error: error associated with the non-concordance between
  the values of two or more variables according to the underlying
  relationship between them.

- Graphic editing: process in which graphics are used to identify
  anomalies in the data. It is recommended to use interactive methods
  that interconnect groups of graphs automatically and retrieve detailed
  records for manual review and editing.

- Troubleshooting: automatic identification process of the fields that
  require editing/correction from the focal point of the country. In
  most cases, an optimization algorithm is used to determine the minimum
  set of fields to be reviewed and corrected by the country focal point
  to ensure that the final record is free of errors.

- Metrics/Error Statistics: mathematical expressions that allow
  analyzing and reporting the different quality errors found. It is
  usual to provide the error rate on individual data items and the
  occurrence of certain types of errors.

- Acceptance rule: norm/condition established from the type of variable
  and its possible values which can be generalized from the
  logical/mathematical point of view applied to each of the variables to
  determine if the results presented in the database to be considered
  correct.

- Check/check rule: logical condition or a constraint to the value of a
  data element that must be met for the data to be considered correct.

- Correction rules: norm/condition established with the purpose of
  making the necessary and sufficient changes so that the values
  available in the response variable meet the requirements of each
  variable to comply with the validation rule(s).

- Cluster Data Review: A method that uses a quality results file in the
  correction preparation process. This approach splits the logs into two
  files, one containing the "good" quality logs and one containing the
  data with errors that can be corrected through an interactive process.

- Automated data review: procedure in which the acceptance rules are
  executed to identify anomalous patterns in a database to be analyzed
  according to the technical requirements of the country and its
  relationship with the regional database.

- Success rate: measure associated with the ability of the Data Editing
  process to generate a database without errors.

- Validation of class attributes: process in which it is verified if the
  class attributes of a variable are equal.

- Validation of completeness: procedure in which it is validated that
  all the data of a record have been collected. The minimum validation
  of completeness compares the count of data completed in each record
  with the questionnaire established by the country.

- Validation of the composition: procedure in which it is validated if
  the structure of a logical unit (for example, -Description of the
  ESAVI) is coherent with its definition.

- Consistency validation: procedure that makes it possible to identify
  if the value of two or more data items in a record is contradictory.

- Data validation: procedure by which it is verified if the values
  reported for a variable come from the given set (finite or infinite)
  of acceptable values. For example, a geographic code (field), say for
  a department in Colombia, can be compared to a table of acceptable
  values for the field.

DATA QUALITY AND PERFORMANCE METRICS

Authors such as Sebastian-Coleman (2013) and Loshin (2011) argue that
performance measurement indicators can be classified into three
hierarchies according to the relative relevance of the type of error
identified. At the lowest level, it is advisable to establish a set of
quality metrics that allows measuring the validity and reliability of
the values reported for each variable in the ESAVIs databases in the
countries. At the second level, the degree of cohesion and concordance
of the values observed in the variables is determined, making it
possible to establish the degree of global conformity of the database of
the countries. These measures make it possible to include the question
skips established in the ESAVIs collection form in each country. At the
last level, the joint performance of the countries' databases in meeting
the quality expectations of the regional system will be quantified.

PAHO proposes the use of key performance indicators (KPIs) to monitor
the quality of ESAVI data at the national and regional levels. ICDs will
depend on:

- The dimensions: conditions that define the main data quality metrics.

- The processes: successive phases established to estimate the quality
  indicators.

- The protocols: specify the management practices to be implemented in
  order to guarantee the reliability of the information available in the
  national databases.

- The model: procedure to evaluate the conformity of the information in
  an operational and systematic way in the countries of the region.

- Maturity: consolidation of the general panorama that allows to improve
  the quality of the data of the regional system through the adoption of
  specific best practices at the national level.

The hierarchy in the performance metrics and their objectives are not
static, that is, they can be adjusted over time to synchronize the
dimensions and solidify the protocols within the governance framework
(1). ICDs must have the following characteristics:

- Identifiability: all records in the ESAVIs databases must allow their
  proper identification both at the national and regional levels. PAHO
  recommends establishing a collection methodology that guarantees
  uniqueness in the identification of the case and the patient
  associated with each report. Likewise, it is of vital importance that
  the information system guarantees the uniqueness of the name of the
  vaccine and the relevant information about it. Examples of possible
  indicators that measure this characteristic are:

  - The percentage of search and matching errors.

  - Number of repeated/duplicate case or patient identifiers.

  It is important to clarify that in some cases the identification
  variables can be standardized through homologation tables that allow a
  record to be corrected if necessary. Within this type of variables
  there are those whose report is made by means of free text (for
  example, the entity that makes the report can be approved through a
  list of health service institutions, regulatory entities, ministries,
  as the case may be).

- Reliability: This characteristic allows evaluating the degree of
  implementation and use of the ESAVIs data quality metrics; it also
  makes it possible to identify transparency in national management
  practices to share or exchange reliable information at the regional
  level. PAHO recommends the structuring and consolidation of an
  automated reporting system with the estimation of the different data
  quality metrics implemented in each of the dimensions established at
  the national level. The absence of reports or their incompleteness
  could be considered as possible underlying problems in the ESAVIs
  information system in the countries.

- Availability: This characteristic makes it possible to identify the
  degree and frequency of access to ESAVIs reports in different
  countries. ICDs should regularly review data integrity, timeliness,
  and response times of country managers to assess this component of
  performance.

- Coherence: The ICDs must be coherent in their definitions, method of
  calculation, information required and form of presentation to
  guarantee an adequate information system. The foregoing implies that
  the defined metrics must guarantee semantic consistency, verify the
  availability of the information sources that comprise it, its
  mathematical structure and control of changes to it.

- Compliance with policies: all established quality metrics must respond
  to national and PAHO policies associated with the privacy of their
  estimates, the security of information sources, and/or the limitations
  of their use. In this sense, the national and regional teams must
  continuously monitor whether the calculation of the ICDs responds to
  the established security policies.

 Quality of data indicators

In practice, the quality control of the national ESAVI databases will
focus on the information collection processes in the countries and the
improvement actions that are established to guarantee the quality of the
information in terms of: completeness, consistency, consistency, and
veracity.

At the national level, the indicators focus on errors and
inconsistencies that are obtained from the study of the reports and
their relationship with the ESAVI collection form in the country; This
analysis will make it possible to check the degree of concordance and
coherence of the data with respect to the national reference and to
generate alerts when the data of one or more variables do not meet the
established quality requirements.

Subsequently, crosses and simple frequencies by variable will be carried
out to analyze the behavior of the information with respect to the
phenomena of the investigation, considering aspects such as content,
integrity, and context to determine the validity of the results
generated.

6.1.1 Quality of Data Collection Indicator (QDCI)

The objective of this indicator is to determine the degree of quality
and reliability of the information sent by the entity that makes the
report. This indicator is measured through the number of inconsistencies
detected in the reports made by it and is defined as the weighted
average of the scores achieved by report in the data quality control.
The foregoing implies that to calculate this indicator, a weighting
variable must be created that allows for the distinction and
contribution of each of the inconsistencies that an ESAVI database may
have.

6.1.2 Specific Quality of Data Indicators

The objective of this set of indicators is to monitor the specific
inconsistencies that may arise in the variables during the ESAVI report
in each country. The following indicators are used for continuous
monitoring, control of the quality of the information and the detection
of anomalies in the records:

- Indicator of the variable support (ISV)

The ISV is a metric that allows quantifying the percentage of times (or
the relative frequency) that a value appears that does not correspond to
the support established by the variable, that is, that the values
collected must correspond to the categories provided for the question.

For example, a value of -5 in the age of a person cannot be a valid
analysis option because it does not correspond to the numerical support
of this variable. However, if a dichotomous variable is considered with
Yes or No response options, then the option "cat" is not valid for the
support of the variable.

The reference level of 0% and the calculation method is:

$$ISV = \left( \frac{Total\ number\ of\ responses\ outside\ the\ support\ of\ the\ variable}{Total\ number\ of\ reports\ in\ the\ database} \right) \times 100$$

Recommendation: If the ISV is greater than 0%, the possible causes of
the error must be determined to establish the necessary preventive
and/or corrective actions so that this type of error is not repeated. In
practice, these errors occur due to typing errors, selection errors,
incorrect handling of the database, among other options.

- Typing error rate (TED)

The TED is the most frequently used indicator when the records are
captured from free text variables to find possible typing and
non-standardization problems. The reference level of this metric is 0%
and the calculation method is:

$$TED = \left( \frac{Total\ number\ of\ typing\ errors}{Total\ number\ of\ reports\ in\ the\ database} \right) \times 100$$

Recommendation: If this metric is greater than 0% in a particular
variable, it is a priority to identify the source of the problem and the
frequency of the event. In the case of accidental typing errors (<3%),
the solution would be to correct the record in situ; while if this
measure is between 3 and 10%, it is recommended to identify the possible
causes of typing errors to take the necessary preventive and/or
corrective actions. In the case of having a variable with a TED > 10%,
it is recommended to establish a coding criterion and/or an automatic
typing system to counteract the problem.

- Question Sequence Indicator (ISP)

The ISP is a metric usually used to detect the quality of the reports
made when the ESAVI collection form has one or more than one question
that determines the sequence of subsequent questions.

For example, if the ESAVI report does not include a report on the
medical history associated with lung diseases, it should not contain
answers to the questions that inquire about the characteristics of this
type of disease. INCLUDE EXAMPLE SEX- PREGNANCY

The reference level of this metric is 0% and the calculation method is:

$$ISP = \left( \frac{Total\ number\ of\ records\ with\ non - matching\ information}{Total\ number\ of\ reports\ in\ the\ database} \right) \times 100$$

Recommendation: If this metric is greater than 0% for any conditioning
variable, an analysis of the source of the problem should be carried out
in the information collection system to take the necessary preventive
and/or corrective actions.

- Indicator of consistency in the responses of a set of variables (ICR)

The ICR is a metric usually used to monitor the consistency of the
information provided by the reporting person or entity.

An example of this type of problem can be given with the age variable
and the date of birth of the patient who presented the ESAVI, which
should coincide for all records.

The reference level of this metric is 0% and the calculation method is:

$$ICR = \left( \frac{Number\ of\ reports\ with\ contradictory\ information}{Total\ number\ of\ reports\ in\ the\ database} \right) \times 100$$

Recommendation: If this metric is greater than 0%, it indicates that
there are filters that are not working properly in the information
collection system in the country, for which reason an analysis of the
source of the problem must be carried out to take preventive actions.
and/or necessary corrections.

- Indicator of consistency in open questions (ICPA)

The ICPA is a metric usually used that seeks to determine if the
information completed in the open questions has a sequence of characters
that indicate words by performing a structural analysis in a sentence.
The reference level of this metric is 0% and the calculation method is:

INCLUDE AN EXAMPLE of ESAVI Description with its MEDdra Code

$$ICPA = \left( \frac{Número\ total\ de\ reportes\ con\ palabras\ coherentes}{Número\ total\ de\ reportes\ en\ la\ base\ de\ datos} \right) \times 100$$

Recommendation: If the ICPA is greater than 0%, an analysis of the
source of the problem must be carried out to take the necessary
preventive and/or corrective actions.

6.2 Preventive actions

All the previously mentioned metrics are strongly associated with a set
of preventive or corrective actions that are delimited transversally in
the collection system of the countries.

1.  Before beginning the collection of information using the country
    form, it is necessary to carry out tests on the validation meshes
    programmed in the information capture software.

2.  Carry out training for reporting entities to help structure the
    structure of the national and regional database from the outset.

3.  The entity that regulates and/or supervises the information reported
    by the reporting entities must validate that the protocols
    established for the completion of the form are complied with.

4.  The regulatory entity must review and validate each of the country
    reports and in case it finds any anomaly or inconsistency it must
    contact the reporting entity to validate the records.

6.3 Corrective actions

The corrective actions that are presented below have a transversal
approach since the metrics previously proposed will be estimated
periodically in the country and at the regional level. Traditional
corrective actions are:

1.  When the regulatory institution/entity identifies a set of recurring
    failures in the completion of the report, it must request a new
    training of the reporting entities.

2.  The regulatory institution/entity must maintain close contact with
    the reporting entities to validate the reports with the errors
    identified and update the reported information correctly.

3.  Reporting entities must contact the patient or perform a detailed
    review of their clinical history when necessary.
